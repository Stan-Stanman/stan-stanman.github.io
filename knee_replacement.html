Case Study: Total Knee Replacement Implant

Author: Salvatore Palumbo

1 Introduction

1.1 Description of the problem

Total Knee Replacement (TKR) is a common surgical procedure for osteoarthritis, with an increasing number of annual procedures. A primary challenge to TKR longevity is the wear of the Ultra-High Molecular Weight Polyethylene (UHMWPE) bearing. Wear particles can lead to adverse biological reactions, such as osteolysis, often requiring costly and risky revision surgery. Computer Aided Engineering (CAE), specifically Finite Element Analysis (FEA), is increasingly vital in orthopedic implant design assessment. FEA offers advantages like reduced development costs, faster time-to-market, and mitigated patient/company risks compared to traditional physical testing. The ISO 14243 standard specifies loading, kinematic, and environmental conditions for wear testing, providing dynamic profiles for forces and motions representative of activities like walking. FEA simulations should replicate these settings for in-silico design evaluation under real conditions.

1.2 Scope and goals

This case study applies FEA to a simplified TKR implant model to conduct a structural analysis on selected load cases from the ISO 14243 standard, rather than a full cyclic wear test. Preventing wear requires understanding the tangential force on the UHMWPE bearing, which depends on pressure distribution. The central goal is to determine and investigate these contact conditions to assess the current TKR implant design and propose improvements. The project tasks included:

  • Literature research on standard protocols and current knowledge.
  • Setting up FE models (mesh convergence, material properties, boundary conditions, contacts) at multiple detail levels.
  • Analysis of the stress field and contact pressure distribution of the UHMWPE insert.
  • Recommending design improvements based on results.

2.1.2 Identification of the center of rotation

The Center of Rotation (CoR) was identified using Ansys Design Modeler by projecting the implant's profile and drawing normals at specific flexion angles (0, 30, 60, 90 degrees). The intersection point of these normals defined the CoR, around which the femur was rotated using a "body transformation."

2.2 Material modeling

The tibial tray and femoral component were modeled using a CoCrMo alloy, and the fixing screw with a titanium alloy (Ti6Al4V ISO 5832-3). These components were considered much stronger than the bearing material and, in line with other studies, were treated as rigid, undeformable bodies to simplify the model.

2.2.1 Elasticity of the bearing

The bearing is made of Ultra-High Molecular Weight Polyethylene (UHMWPE), specifically LENNITE (GUR1050). Elastic properties at $37^{\circ}C$ (human body temperature) were sourced from literature. It was noted that the material exhibits softer behavior under compressive loads, necessitating an increased safety factor when comparing von Mises stress with tensile yield stress.

2.2.2 Plasticity of the bearing

To expedite initial simulations, plasticity was not modeled, as a primary goal was to avoid plastic deformation in the bearing. For the final design, a J-2 plasticity model, calibrated at room temperature, was implemented to verify the model. The temperature dependence of plasticity was not further investigated, given the objective of avoiding plastic deformation.

2.3 Contacts definition

Contacts between the screw and tibia, and screw and bearing, were initially defined as simple linear bonded contacts. The screw's behavior was to be further investigated in a later simulation step. Frictional contacts were defined between the tibia and bearing, and femur and bearing, using a friction coefficient of 0.04, consistent with published literature.

2.4 Boundary conditions definition

Initially, boundary conditions followed the ISO 14243 real testing setup, involving a frictionless support on the tibia and remote displacements for AP motion and tibial rotation on the tibia. However, this led to non-converging simulations. The definitive approach involved fixing the tibia with a tibial support and applying displacements to the femur, inverting the directions from the ISO standard. Axial load was applied to a specific surface on the femur.

3 First Step

3.1 Results and comparison with an analytical model

Initial simulations, even with a coarse mesh, showed very high von Mises stresses exceeding the yield stress, particularly with maximum AP motion. An analytical calculation, approximating the stress to 40 MPa, supported these findings, indicating a clear need for design modification to avoid this issue. High tibial rotation also led to highly localized stresses and femoral component penetration.

3.2 Design improvements

The main cause of high stresses was the particularly convex housing of the femoral component, leading to impact with its housing during large AP displacement. Design changes aimed to reduce this convexity while preserving fundamental curvatures and essential heights. This involved projecting surfaces, creating 2D sketches, and revolving them to obtain surfaces with greater curvature, then extruding to remove excess material. This was done for both AP displacement and tibial rotation directions.

4 Second Step

4.1 Mesh convergence

With results comparable to the yield stress, a mesh convergence study was crucial for the bearing. The refinement covered the entire bearing, as the maximum Mises stress was found internally. While some safety factors were greater than 1.5 (considered conservative for yielding), others (especially for max tibial rotation) were not admissible, indicating further design improvements were needed.

4.2 Second design improvements

For the "Min Tibial rotation" case, further material was cut and the bearing's stiffness reduced by removing material from the middle. For the "Max tibial rotation" case, high stresses were on the other side of the femoral condyle; the design improvement involved cutting material that constrained rotation, without significantly altering shapes or curvatures. These modifications led to the new, definitive design.

5 Final Results

Despite some uncertainty regarding the yield strength definition due to lower compressive values, the results were considered satisfactory, supported by other studies setting the yield strength at 21 MPa. To enhance safety, implementing a highly cross-linked GUR1050 was suggested. In terms of pressure, the final step results never exceeded 25 MPa, which was a significant improvement compared to initial findings.
